Understanding Arbeidsmiljøloven

The Norwegian Working Environment Act (Arbeidsmiljøloven) sets out the legal requirements for work scheduling in Norway. As an employer, you are responsible for ensuring compliance with these rules. Non-compliance can result in fines, legal action, and damage to your business reputation.

This guide covers the key scheduling requirements you need to know. While we've made every effort to be accurate, this is not legal advice. Always consult the official legislation or a legal professional for specific situations.

The 14-Day Rule (§ 10-2)

What it says: Work schedules must be made known to employees at least 14 days before the start of the schedule period.

Key Points:

  • Schedules must be "published" - simply creating them isn't enough
  • The 14 days are calculated from the first shift in the schedule
  • Changes after publication require valid reasons and documentation
  • Employee representatives should be consulted on schedules

Example: If your schedule starts on Monday, January 27th, it must be published by Monday, January 13th at the latest.

Common mistake: Many businesses create schedules on time but forget to formally publish them. Ensure employees are notified when schedules are available.

Rest Periods (§ 10-8)

Employees are entitled to minimum rest periods between shifts and over weekly periods. These rules protect employee health and safety.

Daily Rest

11 hours

Minimum uninterrupted rest between the end of one shift and the start of the next.

Weekly Rest

35 hours

Minimum uninterrupted rest within each rolling 7-day period.

Calculating Rest Periods:

Example - Daily Rest:
Employee ends shift at 22:00 on Monday.
Earliest next shift can start: 09:00 on Tuesday (11 hours later).

Example - Weekly Rest:
Count backwards from any point. Within the previous 7 days, the employee must have had at least one continuous 35-hour rest period.

Violation example: Scheduling an employee for 22:00-06:00 night shift, then 16:00-22:00 the same day. Only 10 hours between shifts - illegal.

Working Hour Limits (§ 10-4)

Norwegian law sets maximum limits on how many hours employees can work in a given period.

Standard Limits:

Per 24 hours 9 hours max
Per 7 days 40 hours max

Note: These are the standard limits. Different limits may apply based on:

  • Collective agreements (tariffavtale)
  • Shift work arrangements
  • Special agreements with employee representatives
  • Industry-specific regulations

Overtime Limits (§ 10-6)

Overtime work is subject to strict limits. These apply to work beyond the agreed working hours, not just beyond 40 hours/week.

Per Week

10h

Per 4 Weeks

25h

Per Year

200h

Important Overtime Rules:

  • Overtime must be necessary and time-limited
  • Employee can refuse if there are valid health or welfare reasons
  • Overtime premium (minimum 40%) must be paid
  • Accurate overtime records must be maintained

Warning: Extended limits (up to 20h/week, 50h/4weeks, 300h/year) require agreement with employee representatives. This must be documented.

Night Work (§ 10-11)

Work performed between 21:00 and 06:00 is considered night work and is subject to additional restrictions.

Night Work is Only Permitted When:

  • It's required by the nature of the work
  • There's a strong business need
  • It's agreed with employee representatives (for regular night work)

Night workers are entitled to health assessments and may have the right to transfer to day work if they develop health problems related to night work.

Arbeidstilsynet Inspections

The Norwegian Labour Inspection Authority (Arbeidstilsynet) can inspect your business at any time. Being prepared is essential.

What Inspectors Will Check:

  • Published schedules and publication dates
  • Actual hours worked vs. scheduled hours
  • Rest period compliance
  • Overtime records and justification
  • Employee representative consultation records

Consequences of Non-Compliance:

  • Improvement orders requiring immediate action
  • Daily fines until compliance is achieved
  • Prohibition of continued operations
  • Criminal prosecution in severe cases
